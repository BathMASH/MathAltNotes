1 Integration

Recall: There are two different types of integral. Suppose we have a function f .

1.
The indefinite integral of f is a function F such that
dF dx = f(x).

We write

F = ∫ fdx.

This defines integration as the inverse of differentiation (antidifferentiation). F is called the antiderivative of f .

2.
The definite integral is defined as the net signed area between f and the horizontal axis between a and b . It is written
∫ abf(x)dx.

You are probably used to using indefinite integrals to evaluate definite integrals, e.g.

∫ 0π∕2 sin(x)dx = [− cos(x)] 0π∕2 = 0 − (−1) = 1.

But why are we allowed to combine definite and indefinite integrals in this way?

1.1 Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

This theorem establishes the astonishing connection between indefinite and definite integrals.

Theorem 1.1.

Let f be a continuous function on u ∈ [a,b] .

Part A.

The function f has an antiderivative g given by

g(x) = ∫ axf(u)du

(signed area between a and x , definite integral).

Part B.

Given any antiderivative h of f ,

∫ abf(u)du = h(b) − h(a) = g(b) − g(a) = ∫ f(x)dxx=b −∫ f(x)dxx=a

We don’t have the formal mathematics required to prove this rigorously, and won’t have until Semester 2 of Analysis 1. Isaac Newton didn’t have that formal mathematics yet either, so we (roughly) followed the method of justification he used in 1669.

Justification of Part A. 

We wish to differentiate

g(x) = ∫ axf(u)du.

Suppose x changes by a small increment Δx . The corresponding change in g is g(x + Δx) − g(x) , so the average rate of change from x to x + Δx is

Δg Δx = g(x + Δx) − g(x) Δx .

As Δx → 0 , this approaches the derivative dg dx .

Now, consider the diagram in figure (2).



Figure 2: The area g(x + Δx) = area   A + area   ΔA

By definition of the definite integral defining g ,

g(x + Δx) = area   A + area   ΔA = g(x) + area   ΔA .

There is a point x̂ with x ≤x̂ ≤ x + Δx such that ΔA = f(x̂)Δx , as shown in figure (2). So

g(x + Δx) − g(x) = area ΔA = f(x̂)Δx

Hence

g(x + Δx) − g(x) Δx = f(x̂)

As Δx → 0 , x̂ → x (since it gets sandwiched between x and x + Δx ), so f(x̂) → f(x) and

dg dx = f(x)

Hence g is an antiderivative for f .

(see Analysis 1 Semester 2 for a rigorous proof). □

Note that this result allows us to differentiate certain functions defined in terms of integrals. Explicitly, the statement “ g is an antiderivative for f ” means that

dg dx = d dx ∫ axf(u)du = f(x).

Notice also that the derivative of g does not depend on the lower limit of the integral, a . If we were to evaluate the integral and write an explicit expression for g (assuming that this is possible), this constant a would only contribute a constant term to that expression; the derivative of that constant term would therefore be 0 .

Justification of Part B. 

Let h be an antiderivative of f .

So

dh dx = f = dg dx ⇒ dh dx − dg dx = 0 ⇒ d dx(h − g) = 0 by linearity of derivative ⇒ h − g = c,constant.

Hence

h = g + c.

Now

∫ abf(x)dx = ∫ axf(u)du x=b = g(x)x=b = g(b)

∫ aaf(x)dx = g(a) = 0,

so

∫ abf(x)dx = g(b) − g(a) = (g(b) + c) − (g(a) + c) = h(b) − h(a)

□

Corollary 1.2.

d dx∫ a(x)b(x)f(u)du = f(b) db dx − f(a)da dx

Proof.  Let g(x) be an antiderivative of f(x) , so

dg dx = f(x)

Then by Fundamental Theorem of Calculus,

∫ a(x)b(x)f(u)du = g(b(x)) − g(a(x))

⇒ d dx∫ a(x)b(x)f(u)du = dg db db dx − dg da da dx (chain rule) = f(b) db dx − f(a)da dx

□

Hyperbolic identities

Hyperbolic functions satisfy identities similar to trigonometric identities.

Examples.

1.
cosh 2(x) − sinh 2(x) = 1 because cosh 2(x) − sinh 2(x) = ex + e−x 2 2 − ex − e−x 2 2 = 1 4 e2x + 2 + e−2x − 1 4 e2x − 2 + e−2x = 1 2 + 1 2 = 1.

Compare this with the trigonometric identity

cos 2(x) + sin 2(x) = 1.

2.
1 − tanh 2(x) = sech 2(x). Using cosh 2(x) − sinh 2(x) = 1 and dividing through by cosh 2(x) gives
cosh 2(x) cosh 2(x) − sinh 2(x) cosh 2(x) = 1 cosh 2(x),

i.e. 1 − tanh 2(x) = sech 2(x).

Compare this with the trigonometric identity

1 + tan 2(x) = sec 2(x).

3.
The hyperbolic addition formula for sinh is
sinh(x + y) = sinh(x) cosh(y) + cosh(x) cosh(y).

The corresponding trigonometric identity is

sin(x + y) = sin(x) cos(y) + cos(x) cos(y).

(See Exercise Sheet 1.)

4.
The hyperbolic addition formula for cosh is
cosh(x + y) = cosh(x) cosh(y) + sinh(x) sinh(y).

The corresponding trigonometric identity is

cos(x + y) = cos(x) cos(y) − sin(x) sin(y).

In fact, Osborn’s Rule states that any trigonometric identity can be converted into a hyperbolic identity by

  • replacing trigonometric functions with their hyperbolic counterparts,
  • swapping the sign of any product of two sinh s.

Note: be careful with the second step, e.g.

tanh(x) = sinh(x) cosh(x),

so tan 2(x) becomes − tanh 2(x) (see Example 2).

This arises because hyperbolic and trigonometric functions are related as follows:

cosh(ix) = cos(x), cosh(x) = cos(ix), sinh(ix) = i sin(x), sinh(x) = −i sin(ix),

where i2 = −1 .

This comes from Euler’s relation:

eiθ = cos(θ) + i sin(θ).

Geometric interpretation

In trigonometric expressions such as sin(θ) , cos(θ) , etc., θ can be interpreted as an angle. Similarly, in sinh(t) and cosh(t) , t can be interpreted as an area.

Since cosh 2(t) − sinh 2(t) = 1 , for any t , the point (x,y) = (cosh(t), sinh(t)) lies on the curve x2 − y2 = 1 . Then t corresponds to twice the area bounded by this curve, the x -axis, and the line from the origin to the point (cosh(t), sinh(t)) , as shown in figure (6).



Figure 6: t corresponds to twice the shaded area

Because of this, the inverse hyperbolic functions are denoted arsinh , arcosh , etc. – “ar” is an abbreviation of “area”.

Differentiating hyperbolic functions

Since hyperbolic functions are defined in terms of the exponential function, they are straightforward to differentiate.

d dx sinh(x) = d dx ex − e−x 2 = ex + e−x 2 = cosh(x)

Similarly,

d dx cosh(x) = sinh(x),

d dx tanh x = 1 cosh 2(x) = sech 2(x).

1.3 Integrating functions involving square roots

Integrals involving functions with square roots often arise in mechanics. In this section, we will investigate methods for integrating them using trigonometric and hyperbolic substitutions.

Recall that integration by substitution is a technique for evaluating integrals involving composite functions, using the formula

∫ f(g(x))dg dxdx = F(g(x)) + c,

derived from the chain rule for differentiation. (For a more detailed reminder of integration by substitution, see the additional notes on the course Moodle page.)

Example.

Evaluate the integral

∫ a2 − x2dx,

where x < a .

The integrand is a semicircle with radius a . To inform which choice of substitution to use, consider figure (7). The coordinates of any point (x,a2 − x2) on the curve can be expressed in terms of θ , the angle between the y -axis and the line segment from the origin to that point, using trigonometric functions.



Figure 7: Finding a substitution to integrate the curve a2 − x2

From the right-angled triangle in figure (7), we see that

x = a sin θ.

We will use this for our substitution. Then θ = arcsin x a , dx dθ = a cos(θ) , and

a2 − x2 = a2 − a2 sin 2 (θ) = a2 cos 2 (θ) = a cos(θ),

using 1 − sin 2(θ) = cos 2(θ) ; alternatively, one can deduce this from the right-angled triangle in figure (7).

Thus the integral is

∫ a2 − x2dx = ∫ a cos(θ) × a cos(θ)dθ = ∫ a2 cos 2(θ)dθ = ∫ a2 2 1 + cos(2θ) dθ = a2 2 θ + a2 4 sin(2θ) + c = a2 2 arcsin x a + a2 4 sin 2 arcsin x a + c.

The second term can be simplified further: using the trigonometric identities

sin(2θ) = 2 sin(θ) cos(θ), and cos(θ) = 1 − sin 2 (θ),

we have

sin 2 arcsin x a = 2 sin arcsin x a cos arcsin x a = 2x a 1 − sin 2 arcsin x a1∕2 = 2x a 1 − x2 a2 .

Thus

∫ a2 − x2dx = a2 2 arcsin x a + a2 4 sin 2 arcsin x a + c. = a2 2 arcsin x a + ax 2 1 − x2 a2 + c = a2 2 arcsin x a + x 2a2 − x2 + c.

For a geometric interpretation of this answer, consider the definite integral

∫ 0ba2 − x2dx = a2 2 arcsin b a + b 2a2 − b2, (1.1)

where 0 < b < a . This definite integral is the area shown in figure (8), divided into two regions, R and S .



Figure 8: Definite integral of the curve a2 − x2

The region R is a sector of a circle of radius a with angle arcsin b a , and therefore has area

the first term in the expression for the definite integral (1.1).

the second term in the expression for the definite integral (1.1).

1.4 Integrating rational functions

In this section, we will investigate methods for integrating rational functions. A rational function is a function of the form

f(x) = a + bx + cx2 + … + dxn p + qx + rx2 + … + sxm.

They can be used to approximate many other functions.

Numerator 1 , denominator linear

f(x) = 1 ax + b = 1 a × a ax + b = 1 a 1 g(x) dg dx where g(x) = ax + b

So

∫ f(x)dx = 1 a ln ax + b + c

Numerator 1 , denominator quadratic
f(x) = 1 ax2 + bx + c

We first consider two simpler cases, then show how the general case can be reduced to one of these.

Examples 1.3.

1.
f(x) = 1 a2 + x2

Substitution: let x = a tan(u) , so that u = arctan x a .

Then

dx du = a sec 2(u),

so

∫ 1 a2 + x2dx = ∫ 1 a2 + a2 tan 2(u) × a sec 2(u)du = ∫ a sec 2(u) a2(1 + tan 2(u))du = ∫ a sec 2(u) a2 sec 2(u)du = ∫ 1 adu = u a + c = 1 a arctan x a + c.
2.
f(x) = 1 a2 − x2 = − 1 x2 − a2

In this case, we have several options:

  • Use partial fractions: f(x) = 1 (a + x)(a − x) = 1 2a 1 a + x + 1 a − x.
  • If |x| < a , use x = a tanh(u) . Then a2 − x2 = a2(1 − tanh 2(u)) = a2 sech 2(u) .
  • If |x| > a , use x = a coth(u) , so a2 − x2 = a2(1 − coth 2(u)) = −a2 cosech 2(u) .

We will not cover the details of the substitutions here, since they are so similar to the tan substitution in Case 1.

General quadratic denominator

Given an integral with a general quadratic denominator,

∫ dx ax2 + bx + c, (1.2)

we perform the following steps:

  • Complete the square in the denominator.
  • Use a substitution to transform this into
    ∫ p u2 ± q2du,

    with p and q constant.

  • If q = 0 , this can be integrated directly.
  • Otherwise, integrate using the method from Case 1 or Case 2 from Examples 1.3.

Example 1.4.

 Evaluate

∫ dx x2 + 4x + 8 = ∫ dx (x + 2)2 + 4.

We have completed the square in the denominator. Now let u = x + 2 , so du dx = 1 . Then

∫ dx (x + 2)2 + 4 = ∫ du u2 + 22 = 1 2 arctan u 2 + c = 1 2 arctan x + 2 2 + c.

For completeness, there follows an argument for evaluating 1.2 for general values of a , b and c . Since this is notationally fiddly, but not substantially more complicated than the previous example, this general argument will not be covered in the lectures.

The denominator is

ax2 + bx + c = a x2 + b ax + c a = a x + b 2a2 + c a − b2 4a2 = a x + b 2a2 + 4ac − b2 4a2 . (1.3)

To simplify the notation, write Δ = 4ac − b2 , which is a constant. We make the substitution u = x + b 2a , so du dx = 1 .

Then, from (1.3),

ax2 + bx + c = a u2 + Δ 4a2

so

∫ dx ax2 + bx + c = 1 a∫ du u2 + Δ 4a2

If Δ > 0 , use tan substitution for 1 q2 + u2 with q = Δ 2a .

If Δ < 0 , use partial fractions or a tanh or coth substitution for − 1 q2 − u2 with q = −Δ 2a .

If Δ = 0 , the integral does not require a substitution.

General quadratic numerator and denominator

Given an integral with a general quadratic numerator and denominator,

∫ px2 + qx + r ax2 + bx + cdx,

we use a series of transformations to break this into expressions that we already know how to integrate.

Example.

Evaluate

∫ 2x2 + 6x + 15 x2 + 4x + 8 dx.

We write f(x) for the integrand. First, we manipulate the integrand to remove the x2 term from the numerator:

f(x) = 2x2 + 6x + 15 x2 + 4x + 8 = 2(x2 + 4x + 8) − 2x − 1 x2 + 4x + 8 = 2 − 2x + 1 x2 + 4x + 8.

We cannot do the same to simplify the linear over quadratic term; however, this would be straightforward to integrate if the numerator was the derivative of the denominator.

Observe: d dx(x2 + 4x + 8) = 2x + 4 , so we write

f(x) = 2 − 2x + 4 − 3 x2 + 4x + 8 = 2 − 2x + 4 x2 + 4x + 8 + 3 (x + 2) + 4.

We have broken f(x) into three terms that we can integrate. Notice that the third term is a multiple of the function from Example 1.4. We get

∫ f(x)dx = 2x − ln(x2 + 4x + 8) + 3 2 arctan x + 2 2 + c.

As before, the fully general case will not be covered in lectures, but is included here for completeness:

In the following examples we look at substitution that does this for integrals involving trigonometric functions. The substitution we will use is not at all obvious substitution – it was described by Michael Spivak (author of one of the recommended books for Analysis 1) as “the world’s sneakiest substitution”.

1.6 Reduction formulae

Recall that integration by parts is a technique for evaluating integrals involving products of functions using the formula

∫ udv dxdx = uv −∫ vdu dxdx,

which is derived from the product rule for differentiation. (For a more detailed reminder of integration by parts, see the additional notes on the course Moodle page.)

Sometimes complicated integrals can be reduced to simpler expressions by successive application of integration by parts, giving a type of formula called a reduction formula.

Examples.

1.
∫ ln(x) ndx , n = 0, 1, 2,…

Write

In = ∫ ln(x) ndx = ∫ 1 × (ln(x))ndx

Let

u = (ln(x))n,dv dx = 1,

so

du dx = n(ln(x))n−1 x ,v = x.

Then, using integration by parts,

In = x(ln(x))n −∫ xn(ln(x))n−1 x dx = x(ln(x))n − n∫ (ln(x))n−1dx In = x(ln(x))n − nI n−1

This formula allows us to evaluate In for a given value of n – provided we also do so for all lower values of n :

I3 = x[ln(x)]3 − 3I 2, I2 = x[ln(x)]2 − 2I 1, I1 = x ln(x) − I0, I0 = ∫ 1dx = x,

so

I1 = x ln(x) − x, I2 = x[ln(x)]2 − 2x ln(x) + 2x, I3 = x[ln(x)]3 − 3x[ln(x)]2 + 6x ln(x) − 6x ln(x) − 6x + C.
2.
In = ∫ sin n(x)dx , n = 0, 1, 2,…

Write this as

In = ∫ sin(x) sin n−1(x)dx

Let

u = sin n−1(x),dv dx = sin(x),

so

du dx = (n − 1) cos(x) sin n−2(x),v = − cos(x).

Then, using integration by parts,

In = − cos(x) sin n−1(x) + ∫ cos(x)(n − 1) cos(x) sin n−2(x)dx = − cos(x) sin n−1(x) + (n − 1) ∫ cos 2(x) sin n−2(x)dx

But cos 2(x) = 1 − sin 2(x)

In = − cos(x) sin n−1(x) + (n − 1) ∫ sin n−2(x)dx − (n − 1) ∫ sin n(x)dx = − cos(x) sin n−1(x) + (n − 1)I n−2 − (n − 1)In.

Rearranging this gives

In = 1 n − cos(x) sin n−1(x) + n − 1 n In−2.

We can use this calculate any In . For example, when n = 3 , we get

I3 = 1 3 − cos(x) sin 2(x) + 2 3I1, I1 = ∫ sin(x)dx = − cos(x) + c, ∴I3 = −1 3 cos(x) sin 2(x) − 2 3 cos(x) + c.

1.7 Arc length and surface areas of revolution

Arc length

We often need to know the length of a curve between two points, e.g. what is the length of the ropes holding Clifton suspension bridge (see Exercise Sheet 3).

Idea. Given a curve y = y(x)

Let S be the arc length and ΔS a short section of it.

By Pythagoras’ Theorem,

ΔS2 ≈ Δx2 + Δy2 ⇒ ΔS Δx2 ≈ 1 + Δy Δx2

As Δx → 0 this becomes an identity

dS dx2 = 1 + dy dx2 ⇒ dS dx = 1 + dy dx2

The arclength between x = a and x = b is then

S(a,b) = ∫ abdS dxdx = ∫ ab1 + dy dx2dx.

Example.

Find the arc length of the graph of the function

y = f(x) = x3 6 + 1 2x

on the interval 1 2, 2 .

Sketch the graph of f(x) for x > 0 :

If x = 0 , y is undefined.

We have y = 0 if x3 6 = − 1 2x ⇒ x4 = −3 , hence never.

As x → 0+ , y →∞.

As x →∞, y →∞ as well.

Also,

So dy dx = 0 when x2 − 1 x2 = 0 ⇒ x4 = 1 ⇒ x = ±1 .
